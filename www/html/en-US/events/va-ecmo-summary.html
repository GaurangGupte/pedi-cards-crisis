Summary
  • Peripheral ECMO
    • > 2 weeks postop AND/OR prior sternotomy
    • Fewer resources necessary
    • Risks
      • Vagus nerve injury
      • Tracheal injury
      • Loss of control of vessels into chest
      • Inadequate flow
    • Requirements
      • Need to know patency of peripheral vessels
      • Usually >20 kg for femoral access, otherwise neck access
      • If neck: need to consider contralateral vein, may require decompression
      • If femoral: need to consider leg viability and consider distal artery perfusion cannula. Vein is usually okay unless clinical evidence of venous obstruction (compartment syndromes)
  • Central ECMO
    • Open chest or sternotomy less than 2 weeks prior
    • Requires PDA control and occasional LV vent placement
    • Risks
      • Bleeding
      • Sepsis
    • For critically unwell patients in which the chest is left open, tourniquets may be left at central cannulation sites to facilitate urgent ECMO cannulation if required later
  • LV vent creation
    • Indications
      • No LV ejection
      • LV distension
      • Lung congestion
    • Options
      • BAS creation in cath lab
      • LV/LA vent (prone to clotting/dislodgement)
